Are batteries the new solar panel?

Solar panels still do their job. The value in the market has moved toward moving power through the day rather than making more of it at noon. That is a question about flexibility, so this is a working note on what a battery actually does, and what it is worth.

1. The shape solar leaves

In summer, solar generates hardest in the middle of the day, when demand is lowest and the price is weakest.

On a clear summer day a solar fleet produces most of its output between roughly 10am and 4pm. Demand runs the other way. Homes and offices draw least in the middle of the day and most in the early evening. The two curves separate, and what sits between them is a midday surplus that has to clear somewhere.

It clears on the wholesale market at the wrong time. Over the second quarter of 2025, daytime power prices in Britain fell below overnight prices for the first time on record, after the sunniest spring on record.1 At the extreme the price turns negative and a generator pays to export.2

For a supplier I read this as a margin problem before a climate one. The solar it has contracted to buy arrives in a glut while the day-ahead price is on the floor, and any sell-back happens at that floor. Revenue per MWh on midday solar is thin, sometimes negative, and the same households are then bought expensive power again at the evening peak. The chart is that summer day: generation overshooting demand from late morning, then collapsing as the peak arrives.

2. What a battery does with it

It moves the midday surplus into the evening, for the cost of a small round-trip loss.

A battery sits between the surplus and the peak. It charges through the cheap midday and overnight hours, holds the energy, and discharges into the dear evening. A modern pack returns about 85 to 92% of what it stores, so the loss on the round trip is the only real cost of the trade. What it earns is the gap between the price it charged at and the price it displaced.

The animation is that single day. The battery fills through the cheap troughs and empties into the evening peak, one cycle. State of charge is the running total of those moves.

3. What it saves a supplier

The saving is the spread it removes, between the cheap charge and the dear peak it avoids.

A supplier buys power in two layers. It locks most of its energy a day ahead, then each half-hour settles afterwards at the imbalance price for being short or long. Those prices stack in cost and volatility. On 9 December 2024 baseload wholesale sat near £93/MWh, the day-ahead peak ran to about £142, roughly 53% above baseload, and imbalance sat on top. In March 2025 imbalance averaged £63/MWh when the system was long and £120 when it was short, with half-hours reaching £207.3

Households use most of their power in those dear peak half-hours, so a supplier's demand-weighted cost sits above the flat baseload it hedged. A battery closes that gap. It charges near the floor and discharges into the peak and the imbalance highs.

The value is not even across the year. It is largest on peak-weighted winter evenings and high-volatility days, when a short system pushes imbalance into the £120 to £200/MWh range. On flat, windy days the spread compresses and the saving shrinks. The playbook is plain: charge in the cheapest overnight and midday-solar half-hours, discharge into the evening peak and short-system imbalance. The supplier covers its own forecast error from storage instead of cashing out, and stacks day-ahead arbitrage on Balancing Mechanism dispatch, where GB batteries priced their energy around 20 to 25% below gas plants across 2024 to 2025.5

The day-ahead peak-to-trough spread a two-hour GB battery could capture ran from under £40/MWh in February 2024 to about £70 that August.6 Net that down by round-trip efficiency and a saving in the mid-tens of pounds per MWh survives. Add imbalance avoidance and stacked Balancing Mechanism value, and two-hour GB batteries benchmarked near £50k/MW/year in 2024 and about £73k over the twelve months to April 2026.5 The mechanics get sharper on a volatile day. When the peak hits £138/MWh while the off-peak falls to negative £33/MWh, you are paid to charge and paid again to discharge, worth about £174/MWh on the energy shifted after a 90% round trip.

4. Who runs the battery, and how it bills

Flexibility only earns if the metering and settlement rules let it. They now do.

Few households will trade a battery themselves. Most will sit inside an aggregator's fleet, or a supplier's. The market rules have moved to make that work, and this is the part of the story I find most underrated. Since 2019 independent aggregators have run as Virtual Lead Parties, bidding flexibility from batteries, EV chargers and heat pumps into the Balancing Mechanism without owning the supply contract.7

Two reforms matter most for a home battery. P375 lets a unit settle on a meter at the asset itself rather than at the property boundary, so a battery's actions can be measured and paid even where the house is not yet on half-hourly settlement.8 P444 then compensates the supplier for energy an aggregator moves, priced on Ofgem's cap methodology, so it is not left short when a third party flexes its customer.9

Underneath sits market-wide half-hourly settlement. From late 2025 it begins settling every meter by the half-hour, scaling toward full coverage in 2027.10 Once a supplier sees consumption at that granularity across its whole book, it can price flexibility, reward it, and dispatch a fleet of home batteries as one asset. That is the point where thousands of small batteries become a power station a supplier can actually use.

So, are batteries the new solar panel? Solar still earns its place on the roof. The money and the market design have moved toward flexibility, and a battery is how a household or a supplier captures it. Given the choice, I would rather own the asset that shifts the power than the one that only makes more of it at noon.
